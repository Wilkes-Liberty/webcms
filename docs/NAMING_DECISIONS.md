### 1.2 Brand voice read — what categories of names will fit

Five voice attributes (§2) translate directly into naming constraints. "Mission-anchored" and "confidence-restrained" mean names must carry weight — no lightness, no speed metaphors, nothing that reads startup or agile-first. "Sovereignty-forward" means names that hint at independence, foundation, and control score higher than names implying delegation, velocity, or cloud-dependence. "Technically literate" means the name does not need to explain itself, but it also should not be opaque to a contracting officer reading a capability statement cold. "Outcome-led" means names that gesture toward what the system *does for the mission* are stronger than names that describe what the engineer configures.

The five messaging pillars (§3) suggest the following register vocabulary that names can borrow from without *copying* (vocabulary is for copy; names are proper nouns that should feel distinct):

- **Mission + control** → fortification, foundation, bastions, keystones, anchors
- **Sovereignty** → the word itself is consumed as a pillar vocabulary term (§3.2) and should NOT become another product name — this frees "sovereign" to remain a brand-voice descriptor rather than a proper name
- **Security-as-enabler** → wardens, custodians, thresholds — names that imply controlled passage, not locked gates
- **Defense/government readiness** → names legible in a formal acquisition document; nothing clever, nothing trendy
- **Force multiplier** → names implying concentrated capability — precision instruments, structural elements, foundational materials

Registers that will work across the portfolio: **structural/architectural** (Keystone, Lintel, Vault, Foundry, Plinth), **material/geological** (Granite, Ledger, Quarry, Schist — non-environmental), **navigational/instrument** (Meridian, Sextant, Alidade, Lodestar, Surveyor), **custodial/civic** (Warden, Charter, Custodian, Keeper), **English-language place-or-feature nouns** (Ridge, Bastion, Bulwark, Rampart). Latin and French register is acceptable in moderation — the current portfolio is *overloaded* (Fortis, Apex, Vigilance, Sovereign all skew Latin/French). The new set should mix in English-native words.

### 4.1 Method and limitations

**Method used:** Targeted web search for active company names, brand registrations, and acquired trademarks in the IT services space. USPTO TESS is JavaScript-rendered and inaccessible via automated web fetch; all results therefore come from web searches for known tech/brand collisions, business directory profiles, and trademark-record sites (Trademarkia, Justia Trademarks).

**Limitation:** This is a research-grade conflict screen, not a legal clearance. All finalists — regardless of finding — require attorney review and formal USPTO TESS search before public use. Results below surface known collisions and risk levels. "Low risk" means no active competitor was found; it does not mean the name is clear.

**Risk levels used:**
- 🔴 `COLLISION` — Active company, registered trademark, or well-known product with this exact name in Class 42 (IT services) or Class 9 (software). Do not advance without attorney guidance.
- 🟠 `HIGH RISK` — Multiple active companies or a major product using the name in adjacent IT/software space. High probability of Class 42 conflict; attorney review essential before use.
- 🟡 `MODERATE RISK` — One or more small active companies in the name space, or strong conceptual association in a related domain. Potentially defensible with attorney guidance.
- 🟢 `LOW RISK` — No active IT/software company or registered trademark found using this name. Proceed to attorney search.

**Disposition codes:**
- ❌ `ELIMINATE` — Remove from finalist consideration. Collision is too significant to advance.
- ⚠ `ADVANCE WITH CAUTION` — Usable if attorney search returns clear; Jeremy should be aware of the risk.
- ✅ `ADVANCE` — Low risk; standard attorney review advised before public use.
